Intervention Impact Map — Okhmatdyt

Intervention Direct effect Second-order effects Risks & dependencies
Paid services framework
Revenue
New revenue stream from self-referring patients
Transparent pricing replaces informal payment system
After-hours use of existing premises and staff
→Accounting load increases — cashier system, invoicing, tax reporting required
→Doctors in paid hours may expect additional compensation — salary model revision needed
→Pricing policy requires legal approval from governing body — new administrative process
Systemic risk
→Mission drift: revenue logic ("more paid patients = more money") can gradually override social mission ("we treat all children"). Organisation transitions from social business to de facto private clinic — destroying the core value proposition.
Critical
Mission drift — no governance mechanism to prevent revenue logic from overriding social mission. Requires explicit policy: paid services are capped as a % of total capacity. Social patients always take priority.
High
Legal framework — paid services must be approved by hospital governing authority. Delay or refusal blocks entire stream.
Medium
Staff resistance — doctors may refuse after-hours paid work without clear compensation model.
Dr. Eleks digitisation
Data infrastructure
Single source of truth for patient records
Eliminates triple manual data entry
Structurally blocks informal payments via scheduling
Auto-generates compliance reports for NHIF
→IT support function needed — maintenance, hardware failures, updates
→Training programme required — ongoing, not one-off, for all new staff
→Doctors blocked from informal income → compensation expectations rise → salary model pressure
→GDPR obligations formalised — data retention policy, access controls, processing agreements needed
→Hardware dependency — computers in each department, reliable internet required
High
Adoption resistance — if senior doctors refuse system, informal workarounds return and shadow process persists.
High
Infrastructure dependency — hardware investment and connectivity outside researcher's control.
Medium
GDPR exposure — digital records require formal data governance framework.
Service culture + incentive system
People & culture
Bonus system ties compensation to patient satisfaction
Service standard defines expected behaviour at each touchpoint
Feedback loop created: patient → management → staff behaviour
→Budget increases — bonus fund requires new approved budget line
→HR policy revision — bonus system must be documented in employment contracts and collective agreement
→Management capacity required — someone must process feedback weekly and manage bonus calculations
→Cultural resistance — long-tenure staff may reject external standard as top-down imposition
Systemic risk
→Internal equity problem: polyclinic staff receive bonuses, hospital staff (surgeons, inpatient teams) do not. Creates perceived unfairness across departments → pressure on administration to extend system hospital-wide → budget impact multiplies significantly → potential inter-departmental conflict.
Critical
Internal equity — bonus limited to polyclinic creates organisational imbalance. Requires decision: extend to all staff (budget x3–5) or create explicit, communicated rationale for the scope boundary.
High
Legal complexity — changes require trade union agreement and collective contract amendment. Timeline unpredictable.
Medium
Perverse incentive — if bonus tied to paid service volume (not satisfaction), staff will push unnecessary paid appointments.
Medium
Sustainability — culture change requires sustained management attention. Leadership change collapses programme.
