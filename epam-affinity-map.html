Affinity Map — People Risk Discovery

Synthesis from 50+ in-depth interviews · EPAM Systems engineering unit · 2018
Themes reconstructed from field notes and interview records. Raw data collected through one-on-one conversations across the unit, conducted over two weeks. Quotes are representative composites — anonymised and paraphrased to protect participant identity.
Three themes from 50+ conversations
Theme 01
The organisation has become abstract
People no longer recognise the company they joined. Strategic messages feel disconnected from daily reality.
I stopped going to town halls. It feels like they're talking about some other company.
They multiplied the processes. Just chaos — press this button, then that one, to get the smallest thing done.
The company doesn't care about us. We're just cogs. If you're useful — great. If not — goodbye.
Pattern Loss of institutional trust. People feel like passengers in an organisation moving in an unknown direction.
Theme 02
The outside feels clearer than the inside
Leaving has become easier to imagine than staying. The job market appears more predictable than the current employer.
Honestly, it's easier to just go through a normal interview somewhere else. At least there everything is clear.
They only remember you exist when you already have an offer from somewhere else. So an offer is the only way?
I'm not planning to leave right now. But I keep my options open. Just in case.
Pattern Passive disengagement. People aren't angry — they're quietly calculating. Exit feels like a rational option, not a last resort.
Theme 03
I am invisible as a person
People feel reduced to their output. Nobody is tracking them as individuals — their growth, their concerns, their context.
I feel like a brick here. They laid me, mortared me in, and I'm supposed to just lie there. But I don't want to just lie there. I want to live.
I don't know who to go to. I'm not sure anyone is actually tracking my situation.
I don't know where I fit in the new structure. Nobody has explained it to me.
Pattern The most painful theme. Not frustration with strategy — a felt absence of human attention. People needed to be seen, not managed.
Central insight — where all three themes converge
People didn't need a better strategy. They needed one person whose job was to remember them.
The structural pivot had removed the management umbrella — a single human point of contact who absorbs organisational noise and maintains individual attention. When that layer disappeared, people felt the organisation as abstract, the future as unclear, and themselves as invisible. Compensation was not the issue. Presence was.
What this changed

This synthesis reframed the intervention entirely. Leadership had assumed the problem was strategic turbulence and frozen compensation — addressable by waiting for the transition to stabilise. The field data showed a structural gap that waiting would not fix: the management layer had been stretched beyond the point where individual attention was possible. The solution had to be structural too.
