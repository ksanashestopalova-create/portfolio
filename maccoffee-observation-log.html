Time-Motion Study — Observation Log

Workplace Researcher · MacCoffee · Stick-packaging line staffing study · 2014
Core design principle: The log is divided into two distinct halves. The left captures sensory reality — what was seen or heard, without interpretation. The right captures analytical work — meaning derived from observation only after the fact. Mixing the two produces biased data. Keeping them separate produces evidence.
Observation log template — per operator, per shift
Left column — What I observe
Sensory reality only. No interpretation. What happened, when, and any raw sensory note.
Right column — What I analyse
Analytical work done after observation. Applied only to completed actions, never in real time.

Why each column exists
Left column — observation fields
Time — establishes how much genuine idle time exists in the machine cycle. Unknown at start of study.
Action — documents the full task sequence. Basis for training programme design: what happens, in what order, how.
Raw note — unfiltered sensory observations only. Grimace, hesitation, verbal reaction. Not interpreted here.
Right column — analytical fields
Outcome — did the action produce the expected result? Identifies where errors occur and whether they represent normal variation or skill gaps.
Safety — did the operator follow safety protocols? Shortcuts here cannot be absorbed into time optimisation — they require additional time allocation.
Quality — ISO 9001 and HACCP compliance. Same logic: quality steps are not optional time.
Difficulty — could a non-operator perform this action? Calibrates training complexity and onboarding timeline.
Why 4 operators — not 1

Conclusions were drawn only after completing the study across 4 different operators across multiple shifts. A single operator's performance — whether exceptional or poor — cannot be generalised. Patterns that appear across all four are systemic. Patterns that appear in one are individual. The staffing recommendation required systemic evidence, not a single data point.
