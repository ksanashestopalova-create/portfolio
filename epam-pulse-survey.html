Design decisions — and why
❓
Why one question?
Rejected Multi-question surveys. Longer surveys measure many things but they also create fatigue and noise. When you're tracking a specific intervention, additional questions dilute the signal.
Chosen One question, sent daily. If the intervention works, this number moves. If it doesn't, it doesn't. The simplicity is intentional — it makes the causal link legible.
📏
Why a 6-point scale — not 5, not 10?
Rejected 10-point scale (like NPS). Range 1–5 is effectively ignored by respondents — they treat it as empty space. The scale only activates from 6 upward. Data clusters at the top and loses resolution.
Rejected 3 or 5-point scale. Odd-numbered scales have a neutral midpoint. Respondents default to it under uncertainty — "2 out of 3" or "3 out of 5" becomes a non-answer, not a signal.
Chosen 6-point scale. No neutral midpoint forces a directional response. A 3 means "below the middle — something is off." A 4 means "above the middle — basically ok." The gap between 3 and 4 carries real meaning and respondents feel it.
🔒
Why fully anonymous — no manager attribution?
Rejected Tracking by manager. Would allow comparison between managers — but would also make respondents self-censor. People know their small team is identifiable even in "anonymous" surveys.
Chosen Full anonymity, unit-wide aggregate only. The goal was to measure whether the intervention worked — not to evaluate individual managers. Trust in the instrument required that people felt safe to answer honestly.
📅
Why daily — for a full month?
Rejected Weekly or bi-weekly pulse. Too infrequent to catch day-to-day variation. A bad Monday and a good Friday average out into noise.
Chosen Daily for 30 days. Captures the texture of the experience — not just an average, but a pattern. If scores dip every Monday and recover by Thursday, that's a workload signal. Consistency across 30 days also eliminates single-event distortion.
How scores were interpreted
Score interpretation guide
1
2
Manager is not present in this person's experience at all. Structural or relationship failure.
→ Immediate follow-up with unit head.
3
Below midpoint. Something is off — frequency, quality of contact, or trust. Person is more disconnected than connected.
→ Flag for qualitative follow-up.
4
Above midpoint. Manager presence is felt more often than not. Baseline acceptable.
→ Monitor for consistency.
5
6
Manager is consistently present and the person feels it. The management umbrella is working.
→ Positive signal. Sustain conditions.
Outcome
30-day result · unit-wide aggregate
≥ 4
The score never dropped below 4 across the entire measurement period. This confirmed that the specific problem identified in research — engineers not feeling the presence of their manager — had been structurally addressed. Aggregate satisfaction metrics (eNPS) improved in parallel, but the pulse survey isolated the causal mechanism: the management umbrella had been restored.
