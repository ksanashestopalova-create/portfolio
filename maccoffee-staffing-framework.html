Staffing Decision Framework

Workplace Researcher · MacCoffee · 1-operator-per-2-machines decision · 2014
What the time-motion study showed
Aggregate finding across 4 operators · multiple shifts
> 30%

Idle time in the 1-operator-per-machine model — consistent across all operators, with no critical safety or quality violations during active periods.

This told us one thing: there is unoccupied time. It did not tell us whether that time could safely absorb a second machine. That required a separate study.
Why the data alone was not enough — risk analysis
Risk 01
Quality failure
Operator may not keep pace with two machines simultaneously, leading to unmonitored output and increased defect rates. Idle time in a single-machine model does not guarantee capacity for a dual-machine model.
Risk 02
Safety breach
Under time pressure, operators may shortcut safety protocols. In a food production environment with HACCP certification, this creates compliance exposure and potential harm to the worker.
Risk 03
Physical load
No data existed on whether dual-machine operation was physically sustainable across a full shift. Ergonomic load, concentration demand, and fatigue accumulation were all unknown variables.
Risk 04
Unknown unknowns
New equipment in a new configuration may produce edge cases not covered by existing instructions or anticipated in planning. These could affect any of the above.
Conclusion from risk analysis
The only responsible path to a staffing recommendation was a controlled pilot study. Observation data created a hypothesis. The pilot would test it — under real conditions, with safeguards in place, and with no production consequences if the hypothesis was wrong.
Controlled pilot study design
Hypothesis
One operator can run two adjacent stick-packaging machines without safety or quality compromise
Test conditions Machines placed in mirrored configuration, side by side. Study conducted using written-off raw materials — rejected coffee stock and defective packaging film — so no production losses occur if the hypothesis fails.
Study design
Participant conditions
Full shift paid at standard rate — no financial pressure
Framed as "equipment testing cycle" — not performance evaluation
Operator briefed that defects are expected and acceptable
Shift supervisor checks in every hour — neutral conversation, 5 minutes
Medical check by facility doctor at end of each shift
Study parameters
n = 4 operators · 1 pilot shift each
Minute-by-minute observation log (same template as time-motion study)
Speed calibrated per operator — no fixed target imposed
Defect rate tracked against acceptable norm
Conclusions drawn only after all 4 operators complete study
Researcher (me)
Action log · timing · operator behaviour
Quality engineer
Product output · defect rate · ISO 9001 compliance
Safety engineer
Protocol adherence · HACCP critical control points · ergonomics
Outcome

All four operators completed dual-machine operation within acceptable quality and safety parameters. Defect rates elevated in the first two shifts — consistent with new workflow learning — and returned to baseline by shift three and four.

The hypothesis was validated. The 1-operator-per-2-machines model was approved for production implementation, with a mandatory supervised onboarding period for all new operators on this line.
