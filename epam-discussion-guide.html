  • Identify: Any unresolved career moments (blocked assessment, pending salary review, project changes)?

Interview areas
00 Opening — build participant profile Establish communication style before content
Profile
Brief self-introduction: who I am, why I'm here, how this conversation will be used.
Not a script — adapted to the person. Aim: make them feel this is for them, not about them.
Profile
"How do you prefer to be addressed?" "How do you feel about this kind of conversation — useful, uncomfortable, neutral?"
Sets up communication register for the whole conversation. Documents: open vs. closed communicator.
Add to participant card after opening: preferred name · communication style (open / guarded / performative) · energy level at start of conversation
01 Project — current work context Surface project satisfaction and workload pressure
Observe
"Tell me about your current project — what you're working on, your role there."
I already have this data. The question surfaces engagement level: does the person light up, go flat, or deflect? Energy and detail in the answer matter more than the content.
Probe
"What's the hardest part of the work right now?"
Probe
"How long have you been on this project? Does the timeline feel right for where you are in your career?"
Long stints on one project can signal either stability or stagnation. Listen for which.
02 Manager relationship — presence and support Core research area: is the management umbrella felt?
Observe
"Who is your resource manager right now?"
I know the answer. I'm watching the reaction — body language, tone, speed of response. A name said with a grimace tells more than any follow-up question.
Probe
"When did you last speak with them? What was it about?"
I have the logged date. The subjective experience of "how long ago" varies person to person — 3 weeks is nothing to some, abandonment to others. Calibrate per person.
Probe
"When something goes wrong on a project — or something feels unclear — who do you go to first?"
If the answer is "no one" or "I figure it out myself" — that's a flag.
Provoke
"We're in the middle of restructuring the unit. If you could switch to a different resource manager, would you? Why or why not?"
Deliberately provocative. Surfaces true feelings about the manager that indirect questions don't reach. Most people won't say "yes" immediately — the hesitation, the hedge, the "well actually..." is the data.
03 Career and development Understand agency and direction
Probe
"Do you have a development plan right now? What does it look like?"
A vague answer ("I'm working towards the next level") vs. a specific one ("I have three skills to close, and my manager and I review them quarterly") reveals how actively the person owns their growth.
Probe
"Do you have a mentor? How did that come about?"
Probe
"How is progress on your assessment going? What's blocking it, if anything?"
For those with a known blocked assessment — approach carefully. The question surfaces whether the person has internalised the block as their failure or the system's.
04 Unit and structural change Surface perception of turbulence and belonging
Probe
"How has the last few months felt in terms of the unit — the changes happening, the direction things are going?"
Open enough not to lead. Listen for: clarity vs. confusion, energy vs. fatigue, "we" language vs. "they" language.
Probe
"Do you feel like you know where you fit in the current structure?"
This was the core finding question. "I'm not sure where I fit" was the dominant signal across the unit.
05 Close — open space Capture what the protocol missed
Open
"Is there anything you wanted to say today that we haven't touched on?"
Always last. The things people bring up unprompted are often the most important things.
After the interview — update participant card

Add: preferred communication frequency · topics they raised unprompted · emotional register throughout · any signals that diverge from manager's pre-brief assessment

Flag for unit head if: person expressed strong desire to change manager · mentioned active external job search signals · described feeling invisible or without a clear advocate
