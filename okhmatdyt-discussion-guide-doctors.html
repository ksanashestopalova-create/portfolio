Doctor & Medical Staff Interviews
Participants
Doctors + nurses across departments
Duration
40–50 min interview + observation
Format
Contextual — in their workspace
Research context & goal
These interviews were conducted to understand the actual clinical workflow — not the intended one. Digitisation (Dr. Eleks) had been introduced but adoption was inconsistent. Before designing a training programme, we needed to see where friction was, what workarounds had developed, and why the system wasn't being used as intended. The interviews were conducted in the doctors' own offices and combined with direct observation during working hours.
Interviewer script — opening
Thank you for making time for me. I know your schedule is demanding, so I'll be as efficient as possible. I'm a researcher working on a project to improve the hospital's service processes — not to evaluate your performance, but to understand how things actually work day to day. I'm especially interested in the documentation and records side of things: what takes time, what's frustrating, and what works well. I'd also like to observe you working briefly if you're comfortable with that. Everything you share is confidential. I'll be taking notes. Do you have any questions before we start?
Observation checklist — watch for during the session
How does the doctor open/access a patient record? Paper card, Dr. Eleks, or both?
Who fills in the card — doctor or nurse? When exactly?
Are there visible workarounds — sticky notes, separate notebooks, printed templates?
How does the doctor write prescriptions and lab referrals? Handwritten slips?
Is Dr. Eleks open on the screen? Is it being used actively or occasionally?
What happens at the end of an appointment — where does the card go?
How does the doctor know who is next? Queue management visible?
Any moment of visible frustration, hesitation, or workaround — note it.
1
Warm-up — Daily work context
~5 min
Goal: build trust, understand role and volume of work
Can you tell me a bit about your role here — what department, how long you've been working at Okhmatdyt, and roughly how many patients you see in a typical day?
  • probeDoes the number vary a lot day to day?
  • probeDo you see mostly new patients or repeat visits?
2
The typical appointment — Workflow walkthrough
~12 min
Goal: map the real workflow step by step — what happens, in what order, with what tools
Can you walk me through a typical patient appointment from start to finish — what you do, in what order, and what tools or documents you use?
  • probeWhat's the first thing you do when a patient comes in?
  • probeWhat information do you need to have in front of you?
  • probeHow do you record what happened during the appointment?
  • probeWhat do you do at the end — what gets written, saved, handed over?
⚠ Let them narrate. Don't assume Dr. Eleks is being used — wait to see if they mention it. If not, probe later.
When you write a prescription or a lab referral — can you show me or describe exactly how you do that?
  • probeIs it always handwritten? Is there a standard format?
  • probeHave you ever had a patient come back because they couldn't read what you wrote?
  • probeDo you ever write the same information in more than one place?
What happens with the patient's medical card after the appointment? Where does it go?
  • probeDo patients take it home with them? Does that ever cause problems?
  • probeHave you ever had a situation where a card was lost or unavailable? What happened?
  • probeWhat information do you actually need from previous visits — and how easy is it to access?
⚠ Key area: paper card system pain. Lost cards, duplication, lack of history access.
3
Digital system — Dr. Eleks usage and barriers
~12 min
Goal: understand actual adoption, friction points, and workarounds — the key research question for training design
Do you use Dr. Eleks in your daily work? Can you show me or describe how?
  • probeWhat do you use it for specifically?
  • probeAre there things you do in Dr. Eleks and things you still do on paper — why the split?
  • probeOn a typical day, how much of your documentation ends up in the system vs on paper?
⚠ Don't ask "why don't you use it more" — that puts them on the defensive. Let them describe what they do and work backwards to the friction.
When you do use Dr. Eleks, where does it slow you down or feel difficult?
  • probeAre there steps that feel unnecessary or repetitive?
  • probeHas it ever caused a problem during an appointment — something that made you switch back to paper?
  • probeWhat would need to change for it to actually save you time rather than add to it?
When you first started using Dr. Eleks, how did you learn to use it? Was there training?
  • probeWas the training useful, or did you mostly figure it out yourself?
  • probeWho do you ask when you're unsure how to do something in the system?
  • probeIf you were designing the training for a new colleague, what would you make sure to include?
⚠ This is the core insight for training design — what do people actually need vs what they were given.
4
Friction & workarounds — What's broken
~8 min
Goal: surface hidden workarounds and systemic inefficiencies
What part of your administrative work takes the most time — things that feel like they shouldn't take as long as they do?
  • probeIs there anything you do repeatedly that feels like it should happen automatically?
  • probeDo you ever enter the same information more than once?
  • probeWhat do you spend time on at the end of the day that takes you away from patients?
Have you developed any personal shortcuts or workarounds — things you do differently from how you were trained, because it works better for you?
  • probeDo other doctors in your department do things similarly?
  • probeHas anyone ever told you that your workaround caused a problem downstream?
⚠ Workarounds are gold — they reveal where the official system fails real work. Frame this as curiosity, not criticism.
5
Patient interaction & service perception
~8 min
Goal: understand doctors' perspective on service quality and patient complaints
From your experience, what do patients and families most often complain about when it comes to their visit here?
  • probeIs it usually about the medical side, or more about the organisation — waiting, reception, paperwork?
  • probeHave you ever had a patient who was frustrated before they even got to you?
  • probeWhat do you usually do in those situations?
If you could change one thing about how the clinic operates — not the medical side, but the organisational side — what would it be?
  • probeIs that something you've raised before? What happened?
  • probeWhat do you think stops that from changing?
Closing script
Thank you — this has been incredibly useful. Before we finish, is there anything about how you work or how the clinic operates that you'd like to add — anything we haven't covered? [pause] One last question: if there were one thing that, if fixed, would make your working day noticeably easier — what would it be? We really appreciate your time. Your input will directly inform how we design any changes to the documentation system and training programme.
